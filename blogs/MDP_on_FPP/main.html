Moderate and Large Deviations in First-Passage Percolation

1. Model

First-passage percolation (FPP) is a fundamental stochastic model that describes the random propagation of an interface in disordered media.

Given a lattice $ \mathbb{Z}^d \quad (d \geq 2), $ assign i.i.d. edge weights $ \tau_e $ representing passage times. For vertices $ \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{Z}^d, $ the first-passage time is defined as:

$$ T(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) := \inf_{\gamma: \mathbf{x} \to \mathbf{y}} \sum_{e \in \gamma} \tau_e, $$

where the infimum is taken over all nearest-neighbor paths $\gamma$ from $\mathbf{x}$ to $\mathbf{y}$.

The time constant in direction $\mathbf{u}$ is defined as $$ \mu(\mathbf{u}) = \lim_{N\to\infty} \frac{T(0, N\mathbf{u})}{N}, $$ provided the limit exists.

The limit shape $B_d$ is given by $$ B_d = \{ \mathbf{u} \in \mathbb{R}^d \mid \mu(\mathbf{u}) \leq 1 \}, $$ which describes the asymptotic geometry of first-passage times.

Uniform Curvature Conjecture

A central conjecture in the theory of FPP is that the boundary $$ \partial \mathcal{B} $$ of the limit shape possesses uniformly positive curvature. In other words, there exists a uniform lower bound on the curvature at all boundary points. This property is expected to be crucial in deriving refined fluctuation estimates and establishing links with the Kardar–Parisi–Zhang (KPZ) universality class.

2. Background and KPZ Universality Class

FPP was introduced by Hammersley and Welsh (1965) and is widely believed to belong to the Kardar–Parisi–Zhang (KPZ) universality class, which predicts:

  • Fluctuation exponent $\chi$.
  • Wandering exponent $\xi$.
  • Scaling relation: $\chi = 2\xi - 1$.

In two-dimensional models, $\chi = 1/3$ is conjectured based on exact results in last-passage percolation (LPP) and extensive numerical evidence.

2.1 KPZ Equation and FPP

Consider the KPZ equation for the random interface height function $h^{KPZ}(t, x)$: $$ \frac{\partial h}{\partial t} = \nu \nabla^2 h + \frac{\lambda}{2} (\nabla h)^2 + \eta(t,x), $$ where $\eta(t,x)$ is space-time white noise. We denote by $h^{\rm KPZ}$ the conjectured solution.

The conjectured connection between FPP and KPZ is expressed as: $$ h^{\rm KPZ}(t, \hat{x}) \overset{d}{=} \lim_{N\to\infty} \frac{T(0, N\mathbf{u} + N^\xi \tilde{\mathbf{x}})-N\mu(\mathbf{u})}{N^\chi}. $$

Here, the vector $\mathbf{x}$ is decomposed as $$ \tilde{\mathbf{x}} = \sum_{i=2}^d \tilde{\mathbf{u}}_i x_i, $$ allowing us to observe the limiting fluctuations.

3. Previous Research on Fluctuation Bounds

  • Kesten (1993): Established an upper bound on the variance, $\mathrm{Var}(T) \leq C N$.
  • Alexander (1997): Provided an improved bound, $\mathrm{Var}(T) \leq C (N \log N)^{1/2}$.
  • Newman–Piza (1995): Derived a lower bound, $\mathrm{Var}(T) \geq c N^{1/4}$ in two dimensions.

4. Concentration Results

Under appropriate geometric and moment assumptions on $\tau_e$, one can derive:

  • Upper-tail concentration inequalities.
  • Lower-tail concentration inequalities.
  • Curvature assumptions on the asymptotic (limit) shape.

These estimates enable more precise assessments of moderate deviations in passage times.

Rough claims for them main results

We study moderate and large deviations in first-passage percolation (FPP) on $\mathbb{Z}^d$ ($d \geq 2$). Let $T(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})$ be the first-passage time and $\mu(\mathbf{u})$ the time constant in direction $\mathbf{u}$.

$$ \mathbb{E}\Bigl[\Bigl|T(\mathbf{0}, N\mathbf{u}) - N\mu(\mathbf{u})\Bigr|\Bigr] \asymp N^{\chi} $$

where $\chi$ quantifies the fluctuation order. Under certain conjectured conditions, we establish that for $t$ large enough:

$$ \mathbb{P}\Bigl(T(\mathbf{0}, N\mathbf{u}) > N\mu(\mathbf{u}) + t N^{\chi}\Bigr) = \exp\Bigl(-t^{\frac{d(1+o(1))}{1-\chi}}\Bigr), $$

$$ \mathbb{P}\Bigl(T(\mathbf{0}, N\mathbf{u}) < N\mu(\mathbf{u}) -t N^{\chi}\Bigr) = \exp\Bigl(-t^{\frac{1+o(1)}{1-\chi}}\Bigr). $$

6. Assumptions

Assumption A (Upper‐Tail Concentration)

There exist constants $\overline{\chi}\in (0,1)$, $\theta_0 > 0$, and $c_0 > 0$ such that for all sufficiently large $K$ and for every exponent $a\in [\overline{\chi},1]$, the following holds. For any $\mathbf{v}\in H_{\mathbf{u}}$ satisfying $$ \lvert K\mathbf{v}-K\mathbf{u}\rvert < K^{\frac{\overline{\chi}+1+c_0}{2}}, $$ we have $$ \mathbb{P}\Bigl(T_{\mathrm{Cyl}}(0, K\mathbf{v}) > K\,\mu(\mathbf{v}) + K^a\Bigr) \le \exp\!\Bigl(-K^{\theta_0 (a-\overline{\chi})}\Bigr). $$ Here, $T_{\mathrm{Cyl}}(0, K\mathbf{v})$ denotes the passage time restricted to a suitably chosen cylinder around the straight-line segment joining $0$ and $K\mathbf{v}$.

Assumption B (Directional Finite Curvature)

There exists a constant $C > 0$ such that for all $$ u_{\perp} \in \langle \tilde{u}_2, \dots, \tilde{u}_d\rangle, $$ the following inequality holds: $$ \mu(u + u_{\perp}) - \mu(u) \le C\,\lvert u_{\perp}\rvert^{2}. $$ This condition controls the boundary geometry of the limit shape near the direction $\mathbf{u}$.

7. Main Results

Theorem 1 (Upper‐Tail Moderate Deviations)

Suppose Assumptions A and B hold. Then for any $\varepsilon>0$ and any $a\in [\overline{\chi}+\varepsilon,1]$, if $N$ is sufficiently large and if $\mathbf{v}\in H_{\mathbf{u}}$ satisfies $$ \lvert N\mathbf{v}-N\mathbf{u}\rvert < N^{\frac{\overline{\chi}+1+\delta}{2}}, $$ for some $\delta>0$, one has $$ \mathbb{P}\Bigl(T(0, N\mathbf{v}) > N\,\mu(\mathbf{v}) + N^a\Bigr) = \exp\!\Bigl(-N^{\frac{d(1-\varepsilon)}{1-\overline{\chi}}(a-\overline{\chi})(1+o(1))}\Bigr) \quad \text{as } N\to\infty. $$ In particular, $$ \mathbb{P}\Bigl(T(0, N\mathbf{u}) > N\,\mu(\mathbf{u}) + N^a\Bigr) = \exp\!\Bigl(-N^{\frac{d(1-\varepsilon)}{1-\overline{\chi}}(a-\overline{\chi})}\Bigr). $$

Theorem 2 (Lower‐Tail Moderate Deviations)

Under analogous lower‐tail concentration assumptions, for any $\varepsilon>0$ and $a\in [\overline{\chi}+\varepsilon,1]$ it follows that $$ \mathbb{P}\Bigl(T(0, N\mathbf{u}) < N\,\mu(\mathbf{u}) - N^a\Bigr) = \exp\!\Bigl(-N^{\frac{1-\varepsilon}{1-\overline{\chi}}(a-\overline{\chi})}\Bigr). $$

8. Large Deviations (Borderline Case $a = 1$)

In the borderline case $a = 1$, classical large deviation principles apply. For suitable bounded‐weight distributions one typically obtains

$$ \mathbb{P}\Bigl(T(0, N\mathbf{u}) > N\,\mu(\mathbf{u}) + N\Bigr) = \exp\Bigl(-N^{d}(1+o(1))\Bigr), $$ $$ \mathbb{P}\Bigl(T(0, N\mathbf{u}) < N\,\mu(\mathbf{u}) - N\Bigr) = \exp\Bigl(-N(1+o(1))\Bigr). $$

9. Sketch of the Proof

Step 1: Initial Concentration. Establish base estimates using directional curvature and variance bounds.

Step 2: Iterative Concentration Improvement. Apply a multi-scale argument to refine these bounds.

Step 3: Matching Upper and Lower Bounds. Use induction on spatial scales to prove that the correct exponents emerge in all directions.

Step 4: Connection to Large Deviations. Relate moderate deviations to the rate function in the large-deviation principle.

10. Discussion

  • Fluctuation exponent $\chi$: Widely believed to be $\chi = 1/3$ in 2D, though a rigorous proof remains open.
  • Curvature assumptions: Ensure the limit shape is well-behaved near the relevant direction $u$.
  • Concentration assumptions: Provide exponential control of deviation probabilities both above and below typical passage times.
